How can the cloud be utilized for IoT and Industry 4.0?

The key focus of industry 4.0 is the interconnectivity of machines and the sharing of data to improve operations as a whole. As more machines and operations utilize IoT solutions, they start to produce large volumes of data. This additional data needs to be transferred, stored and analysed to capture new efficiencies within operations.

Scalability

The cloud computing environment has proven that it can offer the scalability to capture these increased volumes of data as it gets generated from production operations. The biggest companies in the world utilize the cloud to manage enormous traffic volumes and data sets.

Availability

Cloud environments are deployed globally with multiple data centers across the world. This ensures that these systems are always available even if a single area is unavailable such as in the event of a power outage. A combination of on-site infrastructure and cloud infrastructure can ensure that operations are never interrupted and visibility is always maintained.

Flexibility

Cloud service providers constantly maintain and update their services to offer their clients the latest solutions to their problems. Cloud services can also be uniquely tailored according to the needs and scale of each operation. As operations are updated or scaled up/down the cloud environment can quickly adapt to new requirements.

What is the cloud?

It should be clear at this point that the cloud can be very beneficial to an industry 4.0 operation, but what exactly is the cloud? Fundamentally the cloud is just a global network of computers. These computers are grouped into manageable clusters where they are stored and maintained at facilities known as data centers. The image above shows an example of what a datacenter might look like.

Cloud providers run specialized software across all the computers which lets them function like one large computing resource. Providers then allow clients to use a portion of this computing resource at a cost.

A good comparison to cloud providers is electric utility companies. Each power plant (or in our case data center) supplies a resource to consumers which they can purchase at a fixed cost. Multiple power plants are connected through a larger power grid. If there is an issue at one power plant, the others are still able to provide power while the issue is fixed.

Cloud providers function similarly to the example above. They provide computing and IT capabilities at fixed costs to their clients in a similar fashion. Clients do not have to concern themselves with the provisioning and maintenance of the underlying systems as that burden is shifted to the cloud providers.

Advantages of the cloud

Some of the advantages to cloud systems have already been explored in the previous section, but there are other benefits to consider.

Cost saving

The majority of costs associated with provisioning and maintaining IT infrastructure is shifted to the cloud service provider. Cloud services are generally pay-as-you-go which means the upfront cost compared to on-site solutions is much lower. The amount of staff required to maintain cloud solutions compared to on-site solutions is also lower, resulting in comparable or lower operational costs.

Mobility

Since cloud solutions are accessed through the internet, they can be utilized anywhere as long as there is an internet connection available. This allows staff to access cloud systems from remote locations away from the office or while traveling. Achieving something similar with a private on-site solution might require additional engineering effort.

Insight

Cloud providers typically offer a suite of analytics tools to help clients monitor their systems and analyze their data. These tools are often free for all clients that use cloud services and can offer valuable insights into operations that would otherwise have to be paid for.

Disaster Recovery

Cloud solutions offer the ability to duplicate and synchronize your operations to multiple locations in the event that a disaster disrupts the service at the main location. The backup locations can take over and continue to provide services while the main location recovers. Achieving the same level of comprehensive disaster recovery without using cloud solutions can be very complex and expensive to create and maintain.

Loss Prevention

Similar to disaster recovery, the cloud environment offers solutions to ensure that no data stored on devices used during operations is ever lost. By storing data directly in the cloud and accessing it remotely, devices can fail and be replaced without losing any business critical data. In an industry 4.0 application, data generated by IoT devices can be kept in the cloud and still be accessible even when a device or system is no longer operational.

Disadvantages of the cloud

As many benefits as there are to cloud solutions, some considerations need to be made to determine if cloud solutions would be viable for your specific operations and needs.

Downtime

In the event that cloud providers experience outages to the point where they can no longer provide their services, clients can be affected. If the availability of IT infrastructure is critical to operations, it might be better to consider a hybrid approach with on-site redundancies alongside cloud infrastructure.

Privacy Concerns

Despite cloud providers implementing the best industry security standards, there is always a risk that sensitive data could be accessed by unauthorized entities and used for something other than the intended purpose. Specialized services might be required to meet security needs similar to how cloud providers build and maintain isolated data centers for governments.

Reliance on Support

In the event that something goes wrong with using cloud infrastructure, you might be at the mercy of the cloud provider's customer support. Poor support could result in problems taking longer to solve than they typically should. Premium support packages might completely negate any costs saved by moving to the cloud in the first place.

Limitations

Using cloud services might impose certain restrictions on operations that would not exist when using a private on-site solution. Cloud services need to be investigated thoroughly to ensure that any restrictions would not impact operations.

Vendor Lock-in

Migrating to another cloud provider can be very costly if you become very reliant on the services of a single provider. In the event that a provider does something you are unhappy with (such as increasing costs or removing services), the ability to quickly migrate to alternative providers could be crucial to operations continuing smoothly.

Different types of cloud services

Cloud providers typically offer a large variety of services aimed at different applications and industries. These services can broadly be categorized into following fundamental types.

Computation

Any service that uses cloud resources to perform some type of operation, task or analysis can be considered a computational task. Some examples of computation services include:

  • Virtual machines
  • Serverless application platforms
  • Cloud GPUs
  • AI and Machine learning services
  • Data Analysis

All of these services use CPU, Memory and GPU capacity to perform tasks directly related to business operations or on behalf of other cloud services.

Communication

Services in this category are related to the transmission and organization of data within the cloud. Examples of communication services include:

  • API Services
  • Data streaming solutions
  • Message ingestion and delivery services.
  • IoT device connection solutions.
  • Serverless application platforms.
  • Networking services such as firewalls and load balancers.

Communication services are critical to industry 4.0 as they will integrate with devices and systems used in operations to collect data.

Storage

Services that offer any form of data storage belong in this category. Some example of storage services include:

  • Low cost archive storage for data that is accessed infrequently.
  • In-memory storage solutions such as Redis or Memcache.
  • Database services
  • Regular hard-drive disk or solid-state disk storage solutions
  • File sharing and collaboration solutions.
  • Data backup and redundancy services.

Operational

Any services related to the management or monitoring of other services are categorized as operational services. This includes services such as logging, security, configuration, migrations, transformations, ect. These services might use the previous service types to perform their tasks and aid with managing resources actively used for operations.

Researching the available services in each category and selecting the appropriate solution to fit the needs of your operation is critical to utilizing the cloud environment effectively.

BigQuery

What is BigQuery?

As the amount of data keeps constantly, new solutions are needed to analyze larger and larger datasets. BigQuery is a data warehousing solution that allows you to query massive amounts of data by leveraging the processing power of massive data centers.

Large datasets are split into small chunks and stored across thousands of hard drives. When the data is queried, the request is sent out across all the machines housing those hard drives and executed in parallel. The results are then stitched together and the final, combined result is sent back as the answer to the query.

If you are interested in more details about the service itself, feel free to watch this additional video presented at the Google Next 2019 event.

Running Your First Query

Now that we have covered the most important aspects of the BigQuery user interface, we are ready to write and run our first query. For the purpose of this exercise we will provide all the queries for you to copy and paste into the editor.

If you know the SQL language the queries will seem very familiar as BigQuery tries to conform to the latest SQL standard. There is however some syntax that is unique to BigQuery and will not work in other database environments.

SELECT
  lang.name AS Language,
  COUNT(lang.name) AS Count,
FROM bigquery-public-data.github_repos.languages,
UNNEST(language) AS lang
GROUP BY lang.name
ORDER BY Count DESC

You can copy and paste the Query into the editor but wait for the editor to check your query for any errors before you run it. Once the editor is done, you will see a green checkmark on the far right of the screen as shown below.

You will also see how much data your query will process. In BigQuery, you pay for the amount of data you store within the service and the amount of data all your queries have processed in total every billing period (typically monthly).

Note: Google gives every user 1TB of query capacity a month with which you can query any datasets public or private. Our query in this example processed about 57MB of data every time it was run. This means we can run a query of this size approximately 17500 times before we use up our monthly free quota. Query results also get cached so if you frequently run the same query and no new data is added, returning the cached result will not add to your quota.

At this point you can press "Run" and wait for the query to execute. The query we are running looks at the "languages" table in our "github_repos" dataset. The "languages" table documents which programming languages were being used in the 3.3 million public projects available on Github at the time.

The query will go through each row and count the number of times each language was used. Once it is finished running, the bottom half of the screen should change to show you the results. The programming languages are listed from the most to least used and also shows how many projects used each language.

Above the results you will see some tabs with information related to the execution of the query. Job Information contains metadata about the query such as the ID, owner, location, execution dates and run times. The Results and JSON tabs provide the query results in different formats depending on how your viewing preference.

The Execution Details tab contains a step by step breakdown of how the query was executed and the times each step took. This information is mainly used to optimize queries to minimize calculation times and costs, but is beyond the scope of our exercise.

Above these tabs you should see how long the query took to execute and the amount of data that was processed. Typically for this query it should execute in about 1.6 seconds. Processing 3.3 million lines of data in 1.6 seconds is slow and can be outperformed by almost any conventional transactional database solution. Transactional databases aim to return queries within milliseconds and are constantly optimized to maintain those speeds.

BigQuery however, is not a transactional database solution. The value of BigQuery becomes clear when you start dealing with petabytes of data and billions of rows in tables. BigQuery will consistently offer query durations within seconds even at these massive scales. Achieving similar performance in conventional transactional databases would require a lot of engineering effort that might not be possible or cost effective.

The reason behind this is that the cloud environment can scale to accommodate your datasets and query sizes. As your data and queries grow in size, more computing power in data centers can be dedicated to ensure results are generated in reasonable times. Since BigQuery bills according to the size of your query, your costs scale with the size of the operations and the data it produces.

Data Studio

What is Data Studio?

BigQuery is a service that allows you to store and analyze data. Data Studio is a presentation layer with which you can package your results and communicate it to others. Business Intelligence solutions are not a new concept and plenty of products and services have existed on the market for a long time.

A few of these service you might have heard of before include:

  • Microsoft Power BI
  • Tableau
  • Qlik Sense
  • SAS
  • IBM Cognos
  • Oracle BI

Data Studio is Google's offering in the BI world and is completely integrated with their cloud ecosystem. There are no costs associated with DataStudio directly but since is connects to services like BigQuery to fetch results those services will generate costs in the background.

Like with everything we have discussed so far, there are advantages and disadvantages to using specific BI solutions. In the case of Data Studio a key advantage is the integration with all Google cloud services. This exercise will show you how quickly you can create a report and populate it with data from the GCP ecosystem.

This advantage does, however, also have the disadvantage of vendor lock. Data Studio is a proprietary technology of Google which means you become very dependent on them when using it. If you were to build out your reports and dashboard with Data Studio and wanted to move to a different platform at a later date it might require a lot of effort to accomplish.

Vendor lock-in is not a problem unique to Data Studio so be mindful of which services and technologies you decide to use. The ideal solutions are widely adopted technologies that are supported by a variety of different service providers. This ensures you have options when migrating from one provider to another without having to redo your entire system.

Data Studio reports are made up of 4 main components as shown in the image below. Data sets are any form of stored data that might want to process and visualize. In our previous exercise the tables in BigQuery are considered a dataset.

Data sources are sets of results exposed to the Data Studio reporting environment. We can use these data sources to populate tables, charts, graphs or any other visualization options. These datasets can also be filtered to only show certain subsets of the data, for example only showing results for a specific year or month.

Connectors are the bridge between a data set and a data source. Any transformations to the raw data that needs to happen to produce the data source is done inside a connector. In our BigQuery exercise the SQL query we wrote to group projects by the languages they use is an example of a connector.

The final component is the reports themselves. This is all the visualizations and sharing capabilities offered by the Data Studio editor that we will explore in this exercise.

Exploring The Interface

Data Studio comes with an web-based editing environment in which you can create your reports. Being able to edit reports from any computer that has an internet connection and a web browser offers a lot of flexibility and makes it possible for people to collaborate easily on reports.

The first thing you will see is a prompt asking if you want to add data to your report. Since we created the report from the data source we just defined, Data Studio automatically tries to add it to your new report. When you are ready, click the "Add To Report" button.

Sharing Reports

Sharing reports with others is a standard feature in Data Studio. You can choose to print out your report or save it as a PDF if you prefer. Keep in mind that if you use these options you need to configure the report completely before you save it as you cannot change the data once the report is saved.

You can also share your reports as a live web page that people can visit. The live version will continue to update as data gets added. For the other sharing solution you would have to print or save the report to PDF again to include data that was added since the previous time you saved it.

To access the sharing options, select the "Share" button in the top right of the screen. You can share the report with specific people by inviting them via an email or you can create a public link that you can send to others. Feel free to share your report with others once it is finished.

You can also invite others to collaborate on reports with you, allowing multiple people to edit the same report. This can be useful if people work from remote locations and all need to contribute to the same report.

Final Thoughts

Tools like BigQuery and Data Studio allow you to store, process and visualize data quickly without having to deploy and manage IT infrastructure yourself. With basic SQL knowledge you can analyze massive datasets in BigQuery quickly by leveraging the cloud infrastructure Google has developed.

Data Studio offers a way to create live reports that stay up to date as the data used in the reports changes over time. Depending on your needs, a cloud service such as Data Studio can be a cheap and easy to use solution for your business intelligence needs. Always keep in mind that there are limitations to what you can achieve with services like Data Studio and evaluating different solutions is key to using the cloud environment effectively.
